New York City has always been a place where writers drink and drinkers write. From the whiskey-soaked booths where O. Henry penned his masterpieces, to the dim back rooms where Kerouac and Ginsberg argued about God and jazz — the city's literary history is inseparable from its bars. And its bookstores are cathedrals.

This is your guide to both. A crawl through the neighborhoods, the shelves, and the stools where New York's greatest stories were told — or, at least, started.

Greenwich & West Village

The Bohemian Quarter
Bar

White Horse Tavern

567 Hudson St, New York, NY 10014

Established in 1880, it became a 1950s bohemian hub — most famous as the place where Welsh poet Dylan Thomas reportedly drank his final shots of whiskey before his death in 1953. James Baldwin, Jack Kerouac, and Norman Mailer were regulars.

Dylan Thomas · Jack Kerouac · Norman Mailer

Bar

Minetta Tavern

113 MacDougal St, New York, NY 10012

A favorite of the Lost Generation and the Beats. Hemingway, Ezra Pound, and E.E. Cummings were regulars here. Legend says writer Joe Gould — subject of Joseph Mitchell's Joe Gould's Secret — lived out of the booths and received his mail here.

Hemingway · Ezra Pound · E.E. Cummings

Bar

Kettle of Fish

59 Christopher St, New York, NY 10014

The epicenter of the Beat Generation. Jack Kerouac was famously photographed here, and it served as a meeting ground for Allen Ginsberg and a young Bob Dylan, who found his voice in these smoky rooms.

Jack Kerouac · Allen Ginsberg · Bob Dylan

Bookstore

Three Lives & Company

154 W 10th St, New York, NY 10014

Named after the Gertrude Stein novel, this beloved corner bookstore is a sanctuary for writers and publishing professionals. Known for its curated selection and irreplaceable old New York literary atmosphere. One of the city's great institutions.

Named after Gertrude Stein · Est. 1968

East Village & Gramercy

Old New York
Bar

McSorley's Old Ale House

15 E 7th St, New York, NY 10003

Opened in 1854, one of the city's oldest bars. Immortalized by Joseph Mitchell in his legendary New Yorker profiles. E.E. Cummings was a frequent patron; the bar famously didn't allow women until 1970 — a history documented in countless essays and lawsuits.

E.E. Cummings · Joseph Mitchell · Est. 1854

Bar

KGB Bar

85 E 4th St, New York, NY 10003

Located in a former Ukrainian social club, this dim Soviet-themed bar is the modern heartbeat of the NYC literary scene. Its legendary KGB Reading Series has featured Pulitzer winners and debut novelists alike — the city's best place to hear a story told aloud.

KGB Reading Series · Still active today

Bar

Pete's Tavern

129 E 18th St, New York, NY 10003

A Gramercy landmark since 1864. Author O. Henry was a regular and famously wrote his masterpiece "The Gift of the Magi" in the front booth — the bar appears in his stories under the pseudonym "Healy's." The booth is still there.

O. Henry · "The Gift of the Magi" · Est. 1864

Bookstore

The Strand

828 Broadway, New York, NY 10003

Famed for its "18 miles of books," this is the undisputed king of NYC bookstores. Since 1927 it has been the primary source for used, rare, and new titles for the city's intellectual elite. To visit New York without visiting the Strand is to have missed the point.

18 Miles of Books · Est. 1927

Midtown & Upper East Side

The Literary Establishment
Bar

Blue Bar at The Algonquin

59 W 44th St, New York, NY 10036

Home to the Algonquin Round Table of the 1920s — Dorothy Parker, Robert Benchley, and George S. Kaufman met here daily for lunch and devastating wit, eventually founding The New Yorker magazine. The hotel cat's lineage continues to this day.

Dorothy Parker · The New Yorker · Round Table

Bar

Bemelmans Bar at The Carlyle

35 E 76th St, New York, NY 10021

Named after Ludwig Bemelmans, author and illustrator of the Madeline children's books. He painted the whimsical Central Park murals covering the walls — in exchange for room and board at the hotel. The murals remain one of New York's great secrets.

Ludwig Bemelmans · Madeline · Original murals

Bookstore

Argosy Book Store

116 E 59th St, New York, NY 10022

Established in 1925 and still family-owned, this six-story townhouse specializes in antiquarian books, rare maps, and autographs. A genuine time capsule of the city's scholarly history, hiding in plain sight on the Upper East Side.

Antiquarian · Rare Maps · Est. 1925

Bookstore

The Drama Book Shop

266 W 39th St, New York, NY 10018

A hundred-year-old institution for playwrights and theater lovers. Famously saved from closing by Lin-Manuel Miranda, it remains the primary resource for scripts and theatrical literature in the city — and one of Broadway's most beloved gathering places.

Saved by Lin-Manuel Miranda · Scripts & Theater
